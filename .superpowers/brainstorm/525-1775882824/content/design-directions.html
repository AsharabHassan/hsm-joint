A. Dark Luxury Editorial

Deep navy/forest tones. Feels like a luxury brand, not a medical site. The quiz becomes a "diagnostic experience." Hero is dark and cinematic. Content is minimal -- everything pushes toward the quiz. Think: private banking meets Harley Street.

Pros

  • Instantly communicates premium/exclusive
  • Strong contrast with competitors (all light/clinical)
  • Dark backgrounds make gold accents pop
  • Reduces "information overload" feel

Cons

  • Older audience may expect lighter/medical look
  • Dark themes need careful accessibility work
  • May feel too "tech startup" if not executed well

B. Warm Minimal with Embedded Quiz

Light, warm, spacious. The hero IS the quiz -- no separate "start" button, the first question is visible immediately in the hero. Cream/ivory palette with black CTA buttons. Feels like a premium consultation, not a sales page. Restrained, confident, editorial typography.

Pros

  • Quiz visible immediately = higher engagement
  • Warm tones feel welcoming for older audience
  • Minimal content = less bounce
  • The quiz IS the page, not buried below content

Cons

  • Less dramatic first impression
  • Light palettes are common in medical
  • Need strong typography to avoid "bland"

C. Split Hero -- Dark/Light Hybrid

Dark hero with bold headline + urgency messaging (NHS wait times), transitions to warm light below. Combines the premium impact of dark with the warmth of light. Stats cards bridge the transition. Quiz is immediately below the fold. The page "breathes" between dark authority and light reassurance.

Pros

  • Best of both worlds -- impact + warmth
  • The dark/light split creates visual rhythm
  • Urgency messaging works for ad traffic
  • Stats bridge the sections naturally

Cons

  • More complex to execute consistently
  • Risk of feeling disjointed if transitions aren't smooth
  • Two "moods" need careful balance
